Sixty-five degrees with a thirteen-mile-per-hour onshore wind — cool enough to keep the windows cracked without discomfort, warm enough that the chaparral on the inland slopes had that dry, faintly medicinal smell that only comes when the marine layer hasn't fully committed to burning off.

The route shape is point-to-point in name only; the start and end coordinates resolve to the same place, so this was a circuit of some kind — 101.97 kilometers logged over 520 minutes, which works out to a true average of 40.4 km/h. That number tells the real story. The p95 speed touches 109.6 km/h, meaning the occasional open stretch of PCH or Kanan Dume allowed for highway pace, but the bulk of the drive was slow, deliberate, interrupted. Canyon roads do that. You drop to a crawl around a blind hairpin, idle behind a truck making a delivery to one of the ridge properties, then open up again on a straight where the ocean fills the entire left frame.

That figure suggests at least one significant climb — likely inland, up one of the canyons threading back from the coast toward the Santa Monica Mountains interior. Latigo, Encinal, Decker: any of them would produce that kind of cumulative rise over a circuit this length. The partly cloudy sky would have kept the ridge views partially veiled, the higher terrain softened behind moving gray.

The long average relative to the distance implies there were meaningful stops — pulled off at a turnout, most likely, where the shoulder widens and the guardrail gives way to open air above the water. At 65°F with that wind, standing outside the vehicle would have been sharp and clarifying. The kind of pause that resets your sense of scale after an hour of watching the road's white dashes.

Returning to Malibu on the same odometer that left it, the circuit complete, the 792 meters of climbing now fully descended and banked. The clouds had not broken by the end. The wind had not dropped. The coast held its posture.
