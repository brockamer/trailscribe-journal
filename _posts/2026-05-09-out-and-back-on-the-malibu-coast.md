Salt haze, low and still
footfalls measure the shoreline
cloud breaks, then holds grey

Left the trailhead at 63°F with a light onshore breeze barely registering on the skin—three miles an hour, just enough to carry the smell of kelp up from the tideline. The sky was doing that particular Malibu thing where the marine layer thins without ever quite committing to blue. Partly cloudy is the polite term for it.

The route was flat by any serious measure—37 meters of gain over 4.74 kilometers means you're reading every small berm and drainage cut as elevation, because that's all there is. Out-and-back on this stretch means the first half you're running with the coast on one side and the scrub slope on the other, and the second half you're doing it in reverse, which is a different experience than it sounds. The light shifts. The wind, such as it was, moved from cheek to cheek.

Average pace worked out to roughly 8:27 per kilometer—honest, unhurried running. The p95 speed of 12.2 km/h shows up in the data as a handful of short accelerations, probably where the surface firmed up or the grade tilted just enough to invite a longer stride. Nothing sustained. The 43-minute total felt right for the distance, neither pressed nor padded.

Turnaround came at the natural halfway point. Stood there for a moment watching a pelican work a trough parallel to shore, riding the compression lift off a small wave set. The cloud deck held its position the whole session—never burned off, never thickened. Temperature didn't move. By the time the return leg was done the breeze had dropped to nearly nothing and the air felt closer, damper, the way coastal afternoons sometimes settle just before the layer rebuilds overnight.

No remarkable sign on the ground today—the substrate was too compacted near the access path, and the softer margins were churned from weekend foot traffic. Conditions logged, distance closed, back to the start.
