Departed Maumelle in the early morning, the Arkansas River still visible in the rearview as the suburbs thinned and gave way to the rolling hardwood country west of Little Rock. The air was warm at 79°F and a 26-mile-per-hour wind pushed steadily out of the southwest, rocking the vehicle on exposed overpasses. Partly cloudy skies kept the light diffuse and shifting, shadows of cumulus dragging across the road in long gray patches.

The first long leg carried west through the Arkansas River Valley, the terrain compressing between ridgelines before opening dramatically as the Oklahoma border approached. Elevation gain across the full 1,119 kilometers was modest at 1,306 meters, most of it accumulated gradually through eastern Oklahoma and the Texas Panhandle rather than in any single climb. The route was point-to-point, no doubling back, just steady westward progress.

Pace held near 95 km/h on average, which on the open two-lane and interstate stretches felt comfortable and unhurried. The 95th-percentile speed of 126.5 km/h reflects the long uninterrupted highway sections through Oklahoma, where traffic was sparse and the road ran straight to the horizon. Total drive time came to 941 minutes — just under sixteen hours — spread across the full distance.

The Texas Panhandle announced itself with a change in the ground: red clay giving way to pale caliche, the vegetation dropping in stature, the sky widening. Wind turbines appeared on the ridgelines and stood in long rows across the flats, blades turning hard in the persistent southwest wind. The landscape here rewards attention paid to small things — a hawk on a fence post, a dry creek bed cutting under the highway, the color shift in the soil from county to county.

Tucumcari arrived as the light was going amber, the Canadian River breaks visible to the north, the Tucumcari Mountain mesa sitting squat and dark against the western sky. The neon of the old Route 66 motels along the main strip was just beginning to compete with the last of the daylight. Total elevation accumulated through the drive placed the finish well above the start, the high plains of eastern New Mexico sitting noticeably cooler and thinner than the river bottoms of central Arkansas.

The wind had not let up by the time the engine was off. Dust moved across the parking lot in low sheets, and the partly cloudy sky had organized itself into a line of high cirrus pointing southwest. Odometer confirmed 1,118.83 kilometers from driveway to stop.
