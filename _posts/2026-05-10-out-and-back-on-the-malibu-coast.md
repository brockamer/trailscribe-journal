Salt air, thin cloud light
footsteps press the damp shoreline
back the way I came

Pushed off from the Malibu trailhead at a comfortable clip, the morning sitting at sixty-five degrees with a light seven-mile-per-hour wind coming off the water. Partly cloudy skies kept the sun from pressing down too hard, and the air carried that particular mix of kelp and brine that only settles in when the swell is low and the tide is on its way out.

The first kilometer or so felt easy, legs finding their rhythm on terrain that offered only modest elevation change — thirty-seven meters of gain spread across the full four-kilometer effort, enough to register in the calves without demanding anything dramatic. The route traced a straightforward out-and-back line, which meant the second half was familiar ground read in reverse, always a different experience than it sounds.

Pace averaged just under eight kilometers per hour across the thirty-two minutes, with the upper fifth of the effort touching ten-point-three — brief surges on flatter, firmer sections where the footing allowed it. Nothing forced, nothing held back. The wind was light enough to be pleasant rather than a factor, and the cloud cover shifted slowly overhead without ever thickening into anything that changed the light in a meaningful way.

The turnaround came naturally at roughly two kilometers out. Stood for a moment, took stock of the coastline stretching in both directions, noted the way the marine layer was thinning to the south. Then back the way I came, same ground, slightly different legs.
