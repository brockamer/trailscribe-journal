Departed Malibu under a partly cloudy sky, temperature sitting at a comfortable 66°F with a 13-mph onshore breeze pressing in off the Pacific. The light was diffuse and shifting, cloud shadows dragging across the coastal sage and chaparral in slow, deliberate sweeps.

The route covered 100.93 kilometers over 517 minutes — a little more than eight and a half hours behind the wheel — tracing what the metrics confirm as a point-to-point loop that returned to its origin. Elevation gain tallied to 784 meters, modest by mountain standards but accumulated steadily through the canyon climbs and ridge traversals that characterize this stretch of the Santa Monica Mountains corridor.

Average speed held at 39.5 km/h, which tells the real story of this drive: long, slow passages through tight canyon bends, stop signs at rural intersections, and stretches where the road demands attention rather than speed. The p95 figure of 110.1 km/h indicates brief windows of open highway — likely the PCH straightaways where the coast opens up and traffic thins — but those moments were the exception.

Mid-route, the pace slowed considerably. The elevation profile suggests time spent on the inland canyon roads, where grades steepen and switchbacks compress distance into altitude. The breeze that felt mild at sea level would have been less present up in the chaparral, replaced by the dry, still warmth that collects in the folds of the hills through midday.

The return to Malibu closed the loop in the same coastal air that opened it. By the time the odometer confirmed the century mark, the clouds had neither fully committed to overcast nor burned entirely off — just that persistent partly cloudy indecision that keeps the light honest and the temperature from climbing. A long, unhurried circuit of a coastline that doesn't reward rushing.
