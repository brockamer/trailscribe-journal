Dry sage holds the air
feet planted on coastal dust
nothing moves but light

The session log reads like a joke: 0.01 kilometers in eighteen minutes, average speed rounding to zero. No elevation, no meaningful displacement. A loop that barely qualifies as a circle. And yet the tracker was running, the minutes were passing, and I was outside in Malibu under a clear winter sky at 60 degrees with a seven-mile-per-hour breeze coming off the water somewhere below the ridge.

This was not a hike. It was a station. I stood — or crouched, or moved in slow arcs of a few feet — reading ground. The soil here is pale and compacted near the trailhead margin, chalky decomposed granite mixed with coastal clay. There were partial impressions in a soft patch just off the hardpan: something canid, probably coyote, the heel pad broad and the toe spread moderate. No claw drag visible, which suggests a trot rather than a walk. The track was dry and crumbly at the edges, likely laid down the previous evening.

The eighteen minutes went mostly to that one small patch of ground. I circled it. I changed the angle of my crouch to let the low morning sidelight rake across the surface. The 7 mph breeze was just enough to make fine debris skitter at the margins of the print, which told me the track had some age — fresh prints in this soil hold their dust. I photographed with a reference scale, then left it undisturbed.

The air at 60 degrees felt warmer in the sun than the number suggests. No marine layer this morning. The chaparral on the slope above was still, the scrub oak catching the light in that flat, olive-drab way it has when the season is dry. A raven passed once, low, without calling.

The loop back to the start was perhaps thirty meters of walking. The tracker recorded it faithfully as negligible. That is the correct reading. The value of the session was entirely vertical — attention pushed downward into a few square feet of Malibu hillside — rather than horizontal. Distance is not always the point.
